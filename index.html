Research focus

The research agenda is organised around thematic strands that connect organised crime, financial crime, and corporate opacity.

One strand examines criminal infiltration in the legal economy, including how actors with illicit capital enter lawful markets, retain operational positions, and reshape firms, sectors, and competitive dynamics.

A second strand examines beneficial ownership opacity, sanction evasion, and concealed control, with attention to intermediary jurisdictions, legal vehicles, and cross-border ownership chains used to reduce traceability.

Across both strands, the objective is to produce evidence that is analytically robust and useful for AML-oriented risk assessment, sanctions enforcement, and corruption research.

Criminal infiltration in the legal economy

Conceptual and empirical analysis of how criminal actors enter, influence, and exploit legitimate markets, sectors, and business environments.

Sanctions circumvention and concealed control

Study of how sanctioned or high-risk actors preserve influence through ownership chains, intermediaries, legal vehicles, and cross-border corporate structures.

Beneficial ownership and ownership-chain reconstruction

Tracing direct and indirect ownership to estimate control, identify hidden exposure, and reconstruct opaque corporate positions.

Risk indicators, screening, and network methods

Application of proxy-based screening, risk indicators, and network analysis to detect suspicious business patterns at scale.

Methods and data

Methods combine criminological reasoning with structured company data, ownership records, and documentary sources to detect concealed control, high-risk exposure, and criminal participation in lawful markets.

Ownership-chain reconstruction

Rebuilding direct and indirect corporate links to estimate control and identify intermediary layers.

Beneficial ownership analysis

Using ownership information, legal relationships, and control logic to infer who ultimately benefits from or influences a company.

Proxy-based screening

Developing indicators and screening strategies to detect patterns compatible with criminal infiltration, sanctions circumvention, or other forms of concealed high-risk exposure.

Network analysis

Mapping connections among firms, owners, corporate vehicles, and jurisdictions to identify patterns of concentration, intermediation, and indirect control.

Qualitative case-based research

Comparative case studies and process tracing to examine how money-laundering, sanctions-evasion, and related illicit practices evolve across sectors and time.

Interviews and documentary analysis

Use of expert interviews, judicial and regulatory documents, and policy materials to contextualise quantitative findings and interpret operational mechanisms.

AML-oriented risk interpretation

Translating criminological evidence into analytical frames relevant to suspicious patterns, beneficial ownership opacity, and corporate risk assessment.

PhD and doctoral research

PhD in Criminology, Università Cattolica del Sacro Cuore, Milan, 2025.

My doctoral research examined the criminal component of the legal economy, focusing on how illicit and high-risk actors use firms, ownership structures, and legal arrangements to acquire, preserve, and obscure control within lawful markets. It combined criminological analysis with ownership-chain reconstruction, proxy-based screening, and empirical work on structured business data.

Previous academic training includes an MSc (cum laude) in Public Policies, specialising in Security Policies (2021), and a BSc (cum laude) in Statistical Sciences, University of Bologna (2019).
